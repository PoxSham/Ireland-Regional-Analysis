Taxes & Spending · National · 2024

Ireland collected €126bn in taxes in 2024.
One in four euros came from multinationals.

Ireland runs a fiscal surplus — spending less than it takes in — which is unusual among EU countries. That surplus exists largely because corporation tax from a small group of US technology and pharmaceutical companies has flooded the exchequer beyond what policymakers expected or planned for.

That creates a structural problem: a revenue base that is both highly concentrated and potentially fragile. If a small number of multinationals restructure, leave, or face a changed international tax regime, the fiscal position could deteriorate rapidly. Meanwhile, the spending side of the ledger shows a government still struggling to deliver enough housing, healthcare, and public infrastructure despite the windfall.

Where the money comes from

Income tax (including USC) and corporation tax together account for nearly half of all tax revenue. The extraordinary rise of corporation tax — from under 10% of revenues in 2014 to 22% in 2024 — is without precedent among EU economies of comparable size.

Tax revenue by category, 2024 — €bn and % share

Total: €126bn. Corporation tax (€28.1bn, 22.3%) now exceeds VAT as a revenue source. Income tax + USC accounts for the largest single category at 24% of total.

Source: CSO Tax Revenue Statistics 2024 / Revenue Commissioners

Corporation tax concentration risk

The top 3 multinationals pay 46% of all corporation tax receipts. The top 10 pay 57%. Foreign-owned companies account for 88% of all CT revenue. This concentration is exceptional by international standards and represents a significant structural fiscal risk.

The OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax (15%) began taking effect in 2024. While Ireland already applies a 15% rate to in-scope groups, the longer-term effect on profit-booking behaviour remains uncertain. IFAC has warned that the government should treat current CT windfalls as non-recurring.

Where it goes

Total government expenditure reached €116.1bn in 2024. Social protection and health together account for nearly half of all spending. Housing (€8.3bn) and transport (€3.6bn) remain relatively small shares despite the severity of both crises.

Government spending by category, 2024 — €bn

Total expenditure: €116.1bn. Social protection (€27bn) and health (€24.6bn) dominate. The combined debt and EU contribution line (€12.7bn) reflects Ireland's legacy debt burden and EU budget contributions.

Housing (€8.3bn, 7.1% of spending) reflects current-year Exchequer investment plus capital transfers. It does not capture all housing-related activity (e.g. HAP, RAS, and LDA off-balance-sheet commitments).

Source: CSO Government Finance Statistics (GFS) Oct 2025

The debt position

Ireland's gross debt of €215.4bn has fallen steadily as a share of GDP (now 38%) — but this is partly the denominator effect of GDP inflation. Against GNI*, the more honest denominator, debt stands at 67%.

Net debt (after subtracting financial assets held by the Ireland Strategic Investment Fund, the NTMA, and the new future fund) stands at €155bn. Ireland is in a materially strong fiscal position — but that position rests partly on revenue that IFAC considers non-recurring.

Gross national debt, €bn, 2019–2024

Debt peaked at €236bn in 2021 before declining. The fall reflects both repayments and GDP denominator growth. The key risk is a CT revenue reversal that forces deficit spending while debt markets are less accommodating.

Source: CSO Government Finance Statistics Oct 2025 — gross general government debt, €bn
