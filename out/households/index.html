Households & Cost of Living · 2024

Dublin's income advantage disappears when you factor in housing costs.

Disposable income per person is higher in Dublin than anywhere else in Ireland. But rent in Dublin consumes 77% of that income — far above the EU benchmark of 30% for cost-burdened households. After housing costs, the West and South-East offer more residual purchasing power than the capital.

This does not mean people are better off living outside Dublin — wages, job opportunities, and public services are all weaker in most other regions. But it does mean the assumption that Dublin is clearly superior in living standards deserves scrutiny.

The rent burden

Rent data is from the Residential Tenancies Board's Q2 2024 Rent Index — mean new tenancy rents by region. These are new-tenancy figures. Households in existing tenancies often pay less, but face the new-tenancy rate when they move. The rent-to-income ratio uses annual rent against annual CSO disposable income.

Rent-to-income ratio by region, 2024 (%)

Annual new tenancy rent as a percentage of annual disposable income per person. The EU 30% threshold for 'cost-burdened' housing is shown as a reference line.

Rent figures are mean new tenancy rents — not renewal rents. The ratio uses per-person disposable income as the denominator, which understates the burden for single-person households and overstates it for multi-person households sharing a single rental unit.

Source: RTB Rent Index Q2 2024 (mean new tenancy rent) · CSO County Incomes 2024 Table 3.1 (disposable income)

Dublin housing affordability crisis: At 77% rent-to-income, a Dublin renter spending the mean new tenancy rent is allocating more than three-quarters of their disposable income to housing. The EU considers 30% the threshold for housing stress. By that standard, virtually every new Dublin rental household is in housing distress. This is not a cyclical spike — it reflects a structural supply failure that has persisted since 2015.

After-rent purchasing power

Disposable income minus annual rent shows what a renting household has left for everything else. This is an imperfect measure — many households own or have subsidised rents — but it illustrates the structural compression of living standards for renters in high-cost areas.
